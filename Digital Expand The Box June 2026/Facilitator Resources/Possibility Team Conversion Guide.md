# Possibility Team Conversion Guide

| | |
|---|---|
| **For** | The operator and CM, at cohort close |
| **What it is** | The operator-facing companion to Module 11's learner-facing continuation guide: how to help a closing cohort convert into a standing Possibility Team, when to advise against it, and how to frame it in public |
| **Reads alongside** | Module 11 (the learner-facing guide) · [Cohort Calendar Template](Cohort%20Calendar%20Template.md) (the 30/60/90 touchpoints where conversion status is checked) · [Referral List](Referral%20List.md) |

A cohort that ends well often wants to keep going. The Possibility Team is the right vessel for that wanting: a small ongoing group, peer-held, doing PM practice in working contexts. This guide gives the operator the conversion mechanics. The learners get their own version inside Module 11; this file is what the operator holds while they use it.

---

## 1. Canon correction first: Possibility Teams are OPEN

v1's Referral List described Possibility Teams as "by invitation or after Trainer Path entry." **That was wrong, and the wording is corrected in v2.** A Possibility Team is open: anyone can start one, anyone can ask to join one, and no PM credential, trainer status, or invitation is required. Openness is not a detail of the format; it is the format. A team that presents itself as invitation-only has imported a scarcity move into a structure built to be commonly available.

Operator implications:

- Never tell a closing cohort that their team needs permission, certification, or anyone's blessing to exist.
- The team's own public framing must say it is open (section 6).
- A team may still be *small* and may still say "we are full right now, here is when we next open." Capacity is honest; gatekeeping-by-status is not.

## 2. What changes at conversion (say this plainly to the cohort)

The conversion is a real boundary, not a renaming. On one side, the course; on the other, the team.

| | The course (held container) | The Possibility Team (peer-held) |
|---|---|---|
| Holder | A qualified CM with screening, triage SLAs, a backup, and a clinical consult line | The members, jointly. Nobody is on duty. |
| Entry | Five-screen intake, partner agreement, enrollment terms | The team's own declared agreements |
| Safety | Doc 04's full machinery | The members' own equipment, the grounding scripts, the referral list, and the honesty to route out what the team cannot hold |
| Scope | Modules, gated High-intensity work, EHP inside the unlock structure | Practice in working contexts: experiments, witnessing, teach-backs, completion loops. **Not** an EHP venue by default. A team that wants witnessed EHP work names a specific, willing, practiced witness per session, and the trauma carve-out applies exactly as in Module 6. |
| The CM | Present | **Gone.** The CM's absence is the defining structural fact of the team. |

Every member should hear the last row stated without softening. A team that quietly believes the operator is still somehow holding them has not converted; it has just stopped paying.

What carries over: the witness discipline, the three-point reply shape, confidentiality, the four feelings vocabulary, the experiment format, and the relationships themselves. The thoughtware is theirs now. That is what the course was for.

## 3. When NOT to convert (the operator's veto-shaped advice)

The operator cannot forbid graduates from meeting; the content is free and so are they. But the operator's advice carries weight at close, and there are conditions under which the right advice is "not yet" or "not this group":

1. **Unresolved safety routings.** Any member currently paused under the crisis escalation flow, exited for safety, or awaiting a clinician's green light does not join a peer-held team as their next container. Routing them into an unheld group undoes the routing. The team can form without them and openly welcome them later, when the routing resolves. This is the one condition the operator states as a hard line, to the individual privately, never to the group with names attached.
2. **The team is forming to keep receiving care.** If the energy of the conversion conversation is "we don't want to lose being held," the group is asking for a container, not offering each other a team. Point them at the alumni touchpoints, the witness bench (graduates can serve on it), and a future cohort, and let the team idea wait until it is an offer rather than a request.
3. **Fewer than three committed members.** Two people who want to keep exchanging are a continued partnership; bless it as that. A team needs at least three so witnessing can rotate and no pair calcifies into therapist-and-client.
4. **One member is recruiting the others into something else.** A team formed as a funnel for someone's coaching practice, business, or community is a different gameworld wearing the team's name. Name it plainly and decline to assist the framing.

"Not yet" is a complete answer. The 60-day alumni touchpoint exists partly to revisit it.

## 4. The conversion moment (operator steps)

1. **Module 11 raises it; the operator does not pitch it.** The learner guide presents the Possibility Team as one of the named next containers. The operator answers questions and provides this guide's content; the impulse must come from the members.
2. **Ask for a founding declaration, not a sign-up.** Whoever wants the team to exist declares it (to the cohort feed before it closes, or at the end-of-course call): what the team is for, the meeting rhythm, and that it is open. No declaration, no team: a team that nobody will found out loud is condition 2 above.
3. **Hand over cleanly.** The operator gives the founders: this guide's meeting scaffold (section 5), the public-framing language (section 6), the referral list (the team keeps using it), and the course materials links. Then the operator steps back. No standing seat at team meetings, no feed access, no reports.
4. **Check at 30/60/90.** The alumni touchpoints ask, lightly, whether the team is meeting and whether it is still open. That is the operator's entire ongoing role.

## 5. The first three meetings (the scaffold the operator hands over)

Give this to the founders verbatim or adapted. Sixty to ninety minutes per meeting, voice or in person, three to eight people.

**Meeting 1 — Founding.** The team becomes a gameworld on purpose: context, purpose, rules of engagement, entered by choice.
- Check-in round: one feeling, located, with intensity (2 min each).
- The founding declaration, spoken by the founder, then each member declares their own *why I am in*, one sentence, sized to their actual life (3 min each).
- Agree the working agreements out loud. Start from the course's partner agreement, adapted: witness rather than advise · confidence · the immediate-danger protocol stays word for word · tell the team before going silent. Write them down. They are the team's, not the course's.
- Decide rhythm (fortnightly is the durable default), platform, and who convenes the next two meetings (rotate from day one).
- Completion loop to close: *"Are we complete?"* until it is true.

**Meeting 2 — The practice rhythm.** The shape most teams keep for years:
- Check-in round (as above).
- One teach-back: a member teaches one distinction in their own words, five minutes, and is witnessed, not corrected (10 min).
- Experiment reports: each member reports one experiment from their life in the capture format: what I did · what happened · what the next experiment is (3–4 min each).
- One member takes a 10-minute witnessed speaking slot (the Module 8 exchange shape, three-point reply). Rotate the slot each meeting.
- Completion loop to close.

**Meeting 3 — First review.** Same opening and rhythm, shortened, plus:
- A drift check, asked as questions: has advice crept in? Has one person become the helper? Is anyone using the team as their only support? (The last one routes to the referral list, kindly.)
- Recommit or close: each member says plainly whether they are in for the next three months. A team that closes cleanly here, with a completion loop, is a success, not a failure. A gameworld is entered by choice and closed consciously.
- If recommitted: name the next open-door moment: when and how new members can join (section 6).

## 6. Public framing (the words that keep it open)

Whatever the team posts publicly (a line in a PM community space, a note to friends, a message the operator forwards to the next cohort) must carry the openness. A working template:

> We are a Possibility Team: [number] people practicing Possibility Management distinctions in our actual lives: experiments, witnessing, teach-backs. We meet [rhythm, platform/place]. **Possibility Teams are open: no invitation, credential, or prior training is required to start or join one.** We take new members [when/how]; if we are at capacity we will say so and tell you when we next open. To start your own team instead, the course materials are free: [course link].

Two rules inside it: the openness sentence stays, and capacity language ("we are full until March") is the only permitted limit. The operator should decline to circulate any team announcement framed as exclusive, invitation-only, or status-gated, and may quote section 1 of this file as the reason.
