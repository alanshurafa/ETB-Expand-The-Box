# 06 — Group and Community Framework

| | |
|---|---|
| **For** | The operator or community steward deciding to run Expand the Box with a group (from two people up to about a hundred at once), and the learner-curious reader who wants to understand how that holds together |
| **What it is** | The canonical model for running ETB as a group and as a community: the three tiers (dyad, pod, commons), why the safety container does not stretch, the public-accountability mechanic, the rhythm, and the roles |
| **Hierarchy** | **[04 - Container and Gatekeeping Protocol.md](04%20-%20Container%20and%20Gatekeeping%20Protocol.md) is the law.** This document extends the law to group and community scale; where the two could ever disagree, Doc 04 wins |
| **Reads alongside** | [02 - Async Delivery Framework.md](02%20-%20Async%20Delivery%20Framework.md) (the Telegram reference configuration) · [Pod Charter](Facilitator%20Resources/Pod%20Charter.md) · [Telegram Community Operator Guide](Facilitator%20Resources/Telegram%20Community%20Operator%20Guide.md) · [Practice/Practicing in a Group](Practice/Practicing%20in%20a%20Group.md) (the learner-facing version) |

The course already runs as a held cohort and as a sanctioned solo path. This document adds the third shape: a group that does the work together, at its own pace, calling on each other for the partner experiences and the witnessed practices, naming out loud what they are about to do so the naming itself becomes a hook. The design question is not how to make the safety container bigger. It is how to nest containers so a hundred people can be in motion at once without any single container holding more than it can carry.

---

## A. The core decision: fractal containers, not one big container

The audited safety architecture caps a held cohort at 8–12 because that is what one held container of attention can carry (Doc 04 §K.2; the rationale is arithmetic, not caution). A community of a hundred does not get there by stretching one container to a hundred. It gets there by nesting small containers inside a coordinating space that is honest about not being a container at all.

Three tiers, each with its own safety container:

| Tier | Size | What it is | Safety container |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dyad** | 2 | The partner or witness pair: the witness strand of the course, already shipped | The 7-line partner agreement (Doc 04 §B.1), or the recruited-witness adaptation (§B.2) |
| **Pod** | 3–12 | The held group: does the rituals together, witnesses each other's High work on calls, runs the teach-backs | The [Pod Charter](Facilitator%20Resources/Pod%20Charter.md), which extends the 7-line agreement; one Pod Anchor keeps the schedule and the charter (not a facilitator) |
| **Commons** | up to ~100 | The wider community: declarations, loop-closings, finding a witness, module rooms, celebrating Beeps | Space Holders (the §F role); the crisis card and self-screen pinned everywhere. The commons is **not** a container |

Held attention is the scarce resource, and it caps at 8–12 per held container. The dyad and the pod are containers because someone is holding attention inside them: a partner who answers within the SLA, a Pod Anchor who keeps the charter, witnesses who are present for the High work. The commons cannot hold attention at that depth across a hundred people, so it does not pretend to. It coordinates, it declares, it celebrates, and it routes everything heavy down into a container that can hold it.

### Why the commons is not a container

A container is defined by held attention, not by membership or privacy (Doc 04 §H; the holding-context entry in the ledger). A private group of a hundred is still a coordination space, however carefully it is moderated. Calling it a container would be the most dangerous claim in this whole design, because it would invite people to do High work where no one is positioned to hold it. The commons states plainly, in its own pinned text, that it is not a container and not a crisis service. That honesty is the safety mechanism, not a disclaimer bolted onto it.

## B. The law that makes the tiers safe

One rule holds the whole structure together, carried verbatim from the safety audit:

**All High work happens live, on a call, inside a dyad or pod, with a qualified witness. Never over text. Never in the commons.** The EHP (Module 6) and the Demon-locator work (Module 9) are the named cases. The witnessed practices belong in a held container where someone is positioned to catch what surfaces; text strips the breath and the timing that witnessing runs on, and the commons has no one holding.

What the commons is for: coordination, declaration, Low and Medium sharing, finding people, and celebrating the work. What it is not for: anything the course tags High, anything that needs a witness, anything a person could not re-ground from alone.

Every existing gate transfers down to the pod level unchanged. Per-visit consent on the High modules (Doc 03 §I) still re-arms every visit. The 10-second readiness checks still gate the High modules (Doc 04 §D). The witness qualification path (Doc 04 §B.2) still decides who may hold an EHP. Nothing in the move to a group loosens a gate; the gates simply now live inside the pod instead of inside the cohort.

The intake becomes two layers. The commons pins the plain-language self-screen and the crisis card so every arriving member meets them before joining (Doc 03 §A). The pod runs the real gate: pods adopt the deployable [Intake Form](Facilitator%20Resources/Intake%20Form.md), which implements all five screens and the bereavement question (Doc 04 §A). A person can be in the commons on a self-screen; a person doing the High work inside a pod has passed the pod-level gate. The §F.3 note below sequences who runs that gate when there is no CM.

## C. The accountability mechanic: Staking

The course already teaches Declaring as one of the Three Powers (Module 10), and the Day-4 experiment format is already a complete commitment artifact: *what I will do · by when · what I will notice*. Staking is those two things made public. It is not a new mechanic to learn; it is an existing one, done where others can see it.

The no-gamification line holds without strain here (Doc 02 §L): there are no points, no streaks, no leaderboards, no completion races. **Visibility is the mechanism, not score.** Naming your experiment in front of the community is what makes you more likely to run it. The hook is human attention, not a number going up.

The choreography, in order:

1. **Stake.** You post your experiment in the Day-4 format on the Stake Board, before you run it. A template is provided. Posting is staking your word in front of the community. Nothing is scored; the post is the commitment.
2. **Witnessed-by.** Up to two people reply "Witnessed 🕯" to your stake. They are now the two people you report back to: a named, human hook rather than a notification. This is the dyad-tier witness convention surfacing in the commons.
3. **Close the loop, publicly.** The capture-within-ten-minutes discipline (Module 4) becomes a reply to your own stake: what happened, what you noticed. The loop closes where it was opened, in front of the same people who witnessed it.
4. **Beeps celebrated.** A failed experiment is posted as a Beep and celebrated as data. This is already canon: a Beep is information that something did not work, a design specification, not a verdict (the Beep · Shift · Go entry in the ledger). The community treats a posted Beep as a high-value post, because it is the one that moves the work. The [Worked Exemplars Pack](Facilitator%20Resources/Worked%20Exemplars%20Pack.md) gains stake, close, and Beep exemplar posts so people can see what a good one looks like.
5. **Sunday skip-read.** The weekly question from the [Daily Practice Spine](Practice/Daily%20Practice%20Spine.md) gets posted as a thread prompt: ***"What got skipped, and what does the skip say about my Box?"*** Answering publicly is optional; the prompt itself is the ritual. A week of skips is a precise reading of where the Box routes a person around the work, which is exactly the data the course runs on.

The pod rituals stay live, not on the board: the red-pill ceremony, the three teach-backs (Modules 3, 6, 9), and the Module 8 partner exchange happen on pod calls, with the [Audio Companion Recording Kit](Facilitator%20Resources/Audio%20Companion%20Recording%20Kit.md) scripts serving as call scripts.

## D. Rhythm: self-paced and together

Two things hold at once. The course stays self-paced, and the community has a pulse.

- **Gates stay readiness-based, never calendar-based.** This is course law, unchanged (Doc 04 §D). A learner reaches a High module when the readiness check passes, not when a calendar says so. Self-paced is the default, and the commons never overrides it.
- **The weekly heartbeat** gives the "together" feeling without forcing sync: Stake Monday, Loop-close Friday, Skip-read Sunday. People sitting at different modules share the same weekly pulse, so the community feels alive even when nobody is on the same content.
- **Waves are optional.** A Wave is a set of pods starting Module 00 the same week, using the existing [Cohort Calendar Template](Facilitator%20Resources/Cohort%20Calendar%20Template.md). It serves people who want true synchrony. A hundred-person launch is one commons plus eight to twelve pods in a single Wave. Waves are an offer, never a requirement; a person can join the commons and form a pod any week of the year.

## E. The Witness Board

Calling on people is structured, so that "I need a witness" does not dissolve into the feed. The Witness Board is the commons topic where people post structured asks and offers:

> Seeking a witness for a small EHP. Tue or Wed evenings, CET. I have read the witness annex and have the Partner Emergency Card.

The qualification path is stated in the ask, not assumed. A witness for the High work must have the 7-line or Pod Charter agreement with the person, must have read the Doc 03 §F witness annex, and must keep the [Partner Emergency Card](Facilitator%20Resources/Partner%20Emergency%20Card.md) within reach (Doc 04 §B.2). An ask that names "I have read the witness annex" is signaling that the asker knows the bar; an offer is accepted only when the offerer meets it. The [Witness Bench Onboarding](Facilitator%20Resources/Witness%20Bench%20Onboarding.md) protocol generalizes to the commons: the standing bench is simply the people on the board who have qualified and are available.

The High-work-never-over-text law (§B) governs the board absolutely. The board is where a witnessed practice gets *arranged*; the practice itself then happens live, on a call, inside the dyad or pod. The board coordinates the container; it is not the container.

## F. The Space Holder role

A held cohort has a CM (Doc 04, throughout). A commons cannot have a CM in the same sense, because no one can hold a hundred people the way a CM holds eight to twelve. The commons has Space Holders instead: a commons-tier role with the CM's triage instinct but not the CM's held-container responsibility.

### F.1 What a Space Holder is, and is not

A Space Holder reads the commons, triages posts, keeps the crisis routing visible, and routes anything heavy down into a container or out to a referral. A Space Holder is **not** a held-container CM: they do not run intake for the whole community, they do not carry a per-pair pairing tracker, and they do not hold High-week vigilance over individuals. They hold the *space*, which is coordination, not the *container*, which is held attention.

The triage rubric is the CM's, adapted (Doc 04 §F). Three flags, three response windows:

| Flag | What it looks like in the commons | Space Holder action |
|---|---|---|
| **Green** | Normal reflection, a stake, a loop-close, ordinary doubt, frustration with a concept | No action required; acknowledgment optional |
| **Yellow** | A member over their head but functional, partner-conflict surfacing in a post, a life stressor compounding the work, repeated numbing reported | DM within 24 hours; offer a pause, a slower pace, a pod check-in, or a 1:1 referral. Route toward a container |
| **Red** | Suicidal ideation, imminent harm, abuse in progress, a dissociative or psychotic state, a medical emergency | Same day, immediate. Crisis-line numbers in the first message. Hand off to clinician or emergency services. The Space Holder refers; they do not "discuss" a red flag |

The crisis escalation sequence (Doc 04 §G) applies to a Space Holder facing a red flag exactly as it applies to a CM. The commons is not a crisis service, and the Ground and SOS topic says so in its pinned text, with the crisis lines listed first.

### F.2 Staffing minimums

**A commons runs with at least two Space Holders, at roughly one per 35 members.** The minimum of two is structural, not a target: a single Space Holder who sleeps, travels, or falls ill leaves the commons with no one positioned to catch a red flag, and the 15-minute red-flag window does not pause for one person's night. The ratio scales the reading load. A hundred-member commons runs with three Space Holders; thirty-five members can run with the floor of two.

A Space Holder qualifies under a bar adapted from the CM bar (Doc 04 §J): completed ETB, basic mental-health first-aid or equivalent, and a named person to consult for hard judgment calls. A Space Holder is not a casual moderator and not a full CM; the role sits between, and the qualifications match the responsibilities of triage-and-route rather than hold-and-carry.

### F.3 Pod-level intake without a CM

When a community runs without a CM, the gate still has to happen, so it moves to the pod. A person joining the commons meets the pinned self-screen (Doc 03 §A) before they join. A person joining or forming a pod self-screens against the same five screens and adopts the deployable [Intake Form](Facilitator%20Resources/Intake%20Form.md) inside the pod (Doc 04 §A). The Pod Anchor holds the pod to having done this before any High work begins; the Space Holders keep the self-screen and crisis card pinned so the commons-level floor never drops.

This is a real gate, run by the pod rather than waived. A pod that has not done the self-screen and adopted the Intake Form is not ready for the High modules, and the Pod Charter states that as plainly as Doc 04 §K states it for a cohort.

## G. Privacy posture

Stated plainly, in the commons Start Here, so no member assumes more privacy than the space provides:

- A private group is still on a third-party server. Telegram holds the messages, however the group is configured. Members share at the depth they choose, knowing this.
- The Beep! Book stays private by default. A stake is chosen exposure: you decide what goes on the board. The book itself is yours and is never posted.
- Practice data stays in the learner's browser. The interactive tools store everything in local storage and send nothing anywhere; the journal export (Doc 02 §F) is the only copy that leaves the device, and the learner controls it.

The platform-level configuration and the per-component privacy posture live in the [Telegram Community Operator Guide](Facilitator%20Resources/Telegram%20Community%20Operator%20Guide.md) and the reference configuration in Doc 02 §F.2. This section owns the principle: the community states what it can and cannot keep private, and lets the member choose the depth.

## H. The scale table, in one place

| Scale | Tier | Container | Who holds | Intake |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2 | Dyad | 7-line agreement | The partner or recruited witness | Self-screen or cohort intake |
| 3–12 | Pod | Pod Charter | The Pod Anchor (schedule and charter); witnesses for High work | Pod adopts the Intake Form |
| 13–100 | Commons | Not a container | Space Holders (triage and route; min 2, ~1/35) | Pinned self-screen; the real gate is at pod level |

A held cohort of 8–12 with a qualified CM remains the original shape, and it holds more depth per person than any tier below it (Doc 04 §K.2). The pod is the community-scale echo of it: smaller authority, the same gates, held by a Pod Anchor rather than a CM. The commons is the new thing, and its whole job is to stay honest about being coordination, not holding.

---

## I. What this framework does not change

- **The High-work-never-over-text law.** Non-negotiable, carried from the audit. Scaling to a community does not create a text-based shortcut around a witness.
- **Readiness-based gates.** A Wave gives people a shared start; it never converts a readiness gate into a calendar gate.
- **No gamification.** Staking is visibility, not score. There are no points, streaks, leaderboards, or completion races anywhere in the community layer (Doc 02 §L).
- **Doc 04 is the law.** Every gate, consent, and qualification in the cohort design transfers down to the pod intact. This document extends the law to new scales; it does not amend it.
